## 1. Overview

This report presents the Intelligibility Evaluation (Step 7) results of the
Nepali OOV TTS Benchmark. The benchmark tests how well a fine-tuned
Nepali TTS model pronounces Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) words — words containing
character bigrams unseen during training — compared to In-Vocabulary (IV) words.

### Evaluation Protocol
- **Metric:** Intelligibility Error Rate (IER%) — percentage of target words
  rated as not intelligible by a human rater
- **Sentences:** 223 five-word evaluation sentences
- **Words rated:** 1115 (595 OOV, 520 IV)
- **Categories:** ABBR, BRAND, CM, CMPY, GOVT, PROP, NAV
- **Raters:** 1

## 3. Analysis

### Highest OOV Error (ABBR — 56.8%)
Abbreviations show the largest OOV IER at 56.8%, with a gap of
+39.0 pp over IV words. This is expected: abbreviations contain
unusual character sequences (e.g. UNESCO, ATM, API) that are rare in Devanagari
training data.

### Low OOV Error (CM — 9.3%, GOVT — 9.3%)
Code-mixed (CM) and government scheme (GOVT) OOV words achieved lower IER than
their IV counterparts (-13.4 pp and +0.0 pp gap respectively).
This suggests the model has learned reasonable pronunciations for these categories,
possibly due to their frequent occurrence in the training corpus.

### Navigation (NAV — 28.4%)
Navigation place names show elevated OOV IER (28.4%), likely because
Nepali place names contain rare conjunct consonants and sandhi forms.

---

## 4. Conclusion

The fine-tuned model (`v2.0-cosine`) achieves an overall OOV IER of 22.6%
versus 18.1% for IV words, a penalty of +4.5 pp.
The OOV challenge is most severe for abbreviations (ABBR) and navigation terms (NAV),
and least severe for code-mixed (CM) and government scheme (GOVT) words.

These results motivate targeted fine-tuning on OOV-rich data — specifically
abbreviation and navigation corpora — as the next step toward closing the OOV gap.
